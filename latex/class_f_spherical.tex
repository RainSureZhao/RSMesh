\subsubsection*{template$<$class FReal$>$\newline
class FSpherical$<$ FReal $>$}
Spherical coordinate system 

This class is a Spherical position

We consider the spherical coordinate system $(r, \theta, \varphi)$ commonly used in physics. r is the radial distance, $\theta$ the polar/inclination angle and $\varphi$ the azimuthal angle.~\newline
 The {\bfseries{radial distance}} is the Euclidean distance from the origin O to P.~\newline
 The {\bfseries{inclination (or polar angle) }}is the angle between the zenith direction and the line segment OP.~\newline
 The {\bfseries{azimuth (or azimuthal angle)}} is the signed angle measured from the azimuth reference direction to the orthogonal projection of the line segment OP on the reference plane.~\newline


The spherical coordinates of a point can be obtained from its Cartesian coordinates (x, y, z) by the formulae $ \displaystyle r = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2 + z^2}$~\newline
 $ \displaystyle \theta = \displaystyle\arccos\left(\frac{z}{r}\right) $~\newline
 $ \displaystyle \varphi = \displaystyle\arctan\left(\frac{y}{x}\right) $~\newline
 and $\varphi\in[0,2\pi[ $ $ \theta\in[0,\pi]$~\newline


The spherical coordinate system is retrieved from the the spherical coordinates by ~\newline
 $x = r \sin(\theta) \cos(\varphi)$ ~\newline
 $y = r \sin(\theta) \sin(\varphi)$ ~\newline
 $z = r \cos(\theta) $~\newline
 with $\varphi\in[-\pi,\pi[ $ $ \theta\in[0,\pi]$~\newline


This system is defined in p 872 of the paper of Epton and Dembart, SIAM J Sci Comput 1995.~\newline


Even if it can look different from usual expression (where theta and phi are inversed), such expression is used to match the SH expression. See \href{http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_coordinate_system}{\texttt{ http\+://en.\+wikipedia.\+org/wiki/\+Spherical\+\_\+coordinate\+\_\+system}}
